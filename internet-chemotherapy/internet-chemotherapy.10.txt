--[ 1 - Introduction

After I wrote Part 9 of my Internet Chemotherapy posts and shut down the C&C I
ended up side-tracked with new and more constructive projects. I felt the urge 
to keep writing but as IoT botnets did not seem to be a big problem anymore it
felt like it was maybe time to move on. It wasn't clear to me if anyone was 
reading my DeepPaste postings anyway. 

However during Q1 2020 new IoT botnets and exploits made mainstream news and 
this piqued my interest in booting up the C&C along with a few sensors to check 
what the Internet looked like nearly 2 years after my previous janitorial
effort. 


--[ 2 - The Internet Two Years Later

At first it did not seem like there was much new activity at all. A tiny ISP 
in Brazil called Cotesma had a few hundred vulnerable Alcatel-Nokia GPON ONT 
routers which got bricked very quickly (perhaps they got a good deal buying 
used routers from Wind in Italy ;) and for a few days there were elevated 
numbers of vulnerable devices compared to metrics from early July 2018. On the 
incoming scan activity front there also didn't seem to be much new things 
going on. Back in July 2018 the inbound scan counts for port 23 were typically 
over 100K unique src IPs per day, yet in April 2020 the numbers were just over 
40K unique src IPs per day. Port 2323 activity had correspondingly dropped from
over 5000 unique src IPs per day to just over 1000. If anything, the IoT realm 
looked pretty quiet.

There were still a few vulnerable devices online that my sensor network 
attempted to brick. Below is a table of bricking attempts made back in July

payload executions):

The big numbers of TR069 hits in early July 2018 were a side effect of my 
botnet being in active scanning mode (sweeping the more interesting networks of
the Internet). I discuss the difficulties of measuring the effects of such 
payloads in Part 8 of my writeup titled 'The IoT Battlefield: The Where and 
Why' and these numbers should be taken with a grain of salt. On July 3 on the 
other hand the botnet was in passive mode and only listening to incoming scans 
(which it still responded to by carrying out hack-back bricking attempts). 
The metrics of July 3 2018 should thus be relatively comparable with the 
metrics of April 4 2020. 

There was however one big outlier in the 2020 data - the number of devices
vulnerable to the well-known XiongMai telnet backdoor (hardcoded login 
root/xc3511) had increased noticeably since 2018. Considering XiongMai's widely 
publicized recall back in September 2016 it was a little surprising that so 
many new vulnerable devices could have appeared on the Internet since 2018. 

To start to understand the picture better, let's look at which countries these
backdoored XiongMai devices were in. This is what the country of origin

Also notable in the above distribution is what is NOT on the list, which is of
course China (home of XiongMai) itself. 

I wondered if this large number of backdoored XiongMai products in neighboring 
countries could be pure coincidence. One theory which came to mind was that 
maybe these were all just some old devices which had been accidentally exposed 
to the public Internet after unlucky ISP-related updates. This could plausibly 
explain the South Korean anomaly.

As I was busy dealing with some new vulnerabilities (sorry Merit Lilin etc 
owners ;) I did not think about the XiongMai outlier for a while, but the 
XiongMai anomaly would continue to haunt me.


--[ 3 - The XiongMai Backdoor Saga Continues

The re-emergence of such a relatively large number of backdoored XiongMai 
products kept bothering me. I thought I had cleaned most of them off the 
Internet and by the time I shut down my C&C in July 2018 over 1.5 years had 
passed since the widely publicized product recall. I was also puzzled by 
there seemingly being a steady stream of new vulnerable XM devices appearing 
on the Internet, so on a whim I started collecting /proc/version outputs on 
vulnerable products on June 7 2020. Due to the nature of the Xiongmai 
bricking payload and the prevalence of "telnet killers" I did not get a 
return string back from most devices, but over the next three weeks I managed 
to accumulate /proc/version samplings from over 500 backdoored XiongMai 
devices.

As most of you know the /proc/version string contains information about the
firmware build and looks like this:

Linux version 3.0.8 (chenyun@localhost) (gcc version 4.4.1 
  (Hisiliconv100(gcc4.4-290+uclibc0.9.32.1+eabi+linuxpthread)) ) 
   #1 Fri May 29 14:47:10 CST 2015

In the above string we can see that user "chenyun" on a system called 
"localhost" built the stack/kernel on May 29 2015. There is also some info
about the kernel and compiler version that was used.

Something odd immediately struck me about the collected XiongMai device 
/proc/version outputs featuring the hardcoded telnet backdoor. They included 
system build dates AFTER the supposed backdoor recall event in September 2016! 
In other words, devices with the hardcoded telnet backdoor were still being 
created and sold to some countries long after XiongMai claimed they had fixed 
their "mistake". Perhaps even more interestingly, most of the backdoored 
systems built in 2018 ended up in - you guessed it - South Korea. 

I have included the raw /proc/version dumps along with the IP address they 
came from at the end of this posting in case anyone wants to dig into these
themselves. 

Let's look at at some additional details of potential interest in the system
build data. For example, we can note that the usernames of engineers who built 
the backdoored systems are completely different pre-recall and post-recall.

Backdoored system building usernames before recall:

chenyun
danson
jinweiwei
leixinyuan
luzs
root
tongchao

No system builds from Oct 2016 have been encountered yet, perhaps suggesting
that there was a temporary halt in backdoored system builds around the time of 
the XiongMai device recall. 

After the recall, a completely new group of users have continued building 
backdoored systems. The usernames of this small exclusive group were:

wutao        (Nov-Dec 2016, these devices ended up world-wide - and perhaps
              unsurprisingly none of them ended up in China)
zhaojianghua (Dec 2016, these devices ended up in Taiwan)
chenwenrong  (Nov 2016 - Feb 2018, the prolific backdoorer - the 2017 device 
              ended up in Iran, and the 2018 devices all ended up in South 
              Korea)
shaoweidong  (Mar 2018 - Apr 2018, a new name, perhaps taking over from dev
              chenwenrong - these devices ended up in Russia and South Korea)

It is perhaps all too easy to start speculating on conspiracy theories and 
such, but here is how I see it:

* It's difficult to rationally explain how the continued creation of backdoored 
  products could have been a mistake not caught by XiongMai's QA and OQC. To 
  me it looks like the telnet backdoor was deliberately "forgotten" in the XM 
  products intended for certain markets.
  
* Forgetting a known backdoor in a product is less risky than creating a brand 
  new backdoor. The latter would look like the act was deliberate, whereas the 
  former can be chalked off as an innocent mistake by some new employees who 
  did not get the memo or read the news.
  
* The above conclusion sheds new light on the Huawei controversy. Products
  made by XiongMai should probably also be on banlists for sensitive environments. 
  I have a hard time believing that the inclusion of the telnet backdoor wasn't
  deliberate in the post-recall builds.

* It's probably no coincidence that no backdoored system builds were created
  in October 2016, and that the users who built them from November 2016 onward
  were a select small group of trusted engineers separate from the regular
  XM workforce who normally carried out such tasks.
  
* There is a need for more robust IQC by vendors who resell IoT products made 
  in China, and the IQC needs to be an ongoing random sampling of every batch 
  shipment and firmware update. Yes, this adds cost, but on the other hand so 
  does the facilitation of industrial espionage and DDoS.
  
* Friendly security researchers who investigate IoT products for backdoors 
  should not simply look at products sourced from one country, but purchase 
  the same product from a few countries (especially ones that are interesting 
  to the Chinese government), and/or examine firmware updates intended for 
  other languages/countries.

* It's probably no coincidence that South Korea stands out as a major victim 
  of new backdoored XiongMai products. In 2017 there was the whole THAAD 
  crisis, and there has been some uneasy tension between the two countries. 
  South Korea is also a lucrative soft target for industrial espionage.
  
* It's probably also no coincidence that none of the post-recall backdoored 
  XiongMai products ended up in mainland China itself.
  
* It's perhaps also no coincidence that backdoored firmwares with a build date
  later than April 2018 have not been found yet. As most of you probably 
  recall, NDAA 2019 (which banned the federal government from purchasing
  equipment from Dahua, Hytera and Hikvision due to security concerns)
  would have no doubt made XiongMai's top brass nervous around that time.

Back in 2016 there was discussion over whether or not XiongMai's telnet 
backdoor was deliberate or not, and then I felt that incompetence could have 
been a reasonable explanation for its existence. However now I'm not so sure. 
The new evidence suggests that the backdoor was preserved for devices shipping 
to neighboring countries of interest to the Chinese government, and that a
small eclusive group of XM engineers were assigned to create these 'dirty' 
stack builds. These backdoored builds have seemingly not ended up in China 
itself. Given the above I am finding it hard believe that the influx of a 
large number of vulnerable XM devices on South Korean networks since 2018 
was an accident. It looks like a deliberate act of sabotage and espionage.

The dump of /proc/version outputs at the end of this posting will hopefully 
add some credibility to my claims. These might also give researchers clues on 
what to look for if they wanted to validate my findings further. 


--[ 4 - Parting (?) thoughts

Back in April 2020 I intended to do a quick review of the state of the 
Internet, and as things currently look pretty stable I'll probably shut down 
my C&C and go back into hibernation. I'll leave my sensors running and they 
will probably provide some added safety for a few months. I have not decided 
on any next steps for now. Who knows, maybe I will find some other topics 
interesting enough to write about in the future. 

* Perhaps the Treck TCP stack vulnerability will result in something 
  interesting down the road? I have not looked at this one closely yet. 
  Maybe I should?
  
* Perhaps I should publish some of my source code? Would it make any difference
  now? I am undecided.

* Maybe I should write more about my work on the SSH side and the taking over 
  of various ISP networks? I still seem to have access to a good amount of them 
  2-3 years later and this bothers me. Why don't the ISPs notice or care? 
  
Whatever happens next, I wish to thank all of you who have done work to improve
the safety of the Internet as a whole. Much has been achieved since 2016 and
the IoT side looks reasonably healthy for now.
